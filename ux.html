Project Name

Making Everyday Saving Easier: A Human-Centered Redesign

Problem Statement

As digital tools take on more complex roles in day-to-day workflows, users expect platforms to be intuitive, responsive, and easy to navigate. In this project, I was asked to evaluate an existing interface that had become difficult for users to navigate. Key actions were hidden behind unclear menus, layouts lacked consistency, and users received little feedback when completing tasks. These design gaps led to frequent errors, low task completion, and frustration across multiple user groups. Without a shared understanding of the end-to-end journey, teams struggled to prioritize improvements or make user-driven decisions.

Objectives

  • Identify core user pain points through stakeholder interviews and journey mapping
  • Visualize the current user flow and highlight friction points using Miro
  • Prototype interface improvements in Figma to address navigation and clarity issues
  • Present a redesigned user flow and interface to guide future development decisions

Methodology

To guide the redesign, I applied a Design Thinking framework supported by quantitative and qualitative research tools

Empathize

I began by conducting stakeholder interviews and identifying early frustrations with the current interface. This initial discovery phase helped surface broad usability challenges and business goals.

Define

To clarify user needs, I designed and distributed a survey that received over 120 responses from users across different occupations and age groups. Questions focused on confidence during goal setup, perceived clarity of options like "round-ups" and "funding rules", and whether the app offered tailored savings suggestions.

View the full survey here.

A pattern emerged: students and part-time workers consistently reported confusion around language and lack of feedback. Spreadsheet analysis confirmed that users who selected "Not confident" also frequently chose "Understanding the options" as the hardest part of the process. These insights informed the creation of two persona's Adam and Fatima. Both their goals and pain points shaped the rest of the design process.

Ideate

Using insights from persona journeys and the survey results, I facilitated a sketching sprint and prioritized concepts that improved clarity, language, and flow. Ideas were grouped into themes: navigation, visual guidance, and confirmation feedback.

Prototype

I translated the top ideas into mid-fidelity wireframes using Figma. These included a progress bar to anchor users in the flow, simplified terminology (e.g., “auto-save” instead of “funding rule”), and a visual goal summary at the end of the setup. An interactive prototype allowed for real-time demonstration of the improved experience.

Test & Iterate

I shared the prototype with peers for usability feedback. Revisions included stronger button hierarchy and step-by-step guidance for auto-save options. Additional microcopy was added to reduce uncertainty.

Present

The final deliverables were shared through a structured PowerPoint presentation, walking stakeholders through the original journey map, persona-driven insights, design rationale, and visual redesign—helping align next steps with user needs and business strategy.
