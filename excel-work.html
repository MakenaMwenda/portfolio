Project Name

CIBC Branch Analytics: Understanding Product Uptake and Customer Value

Overview

As part of a simulated CIBC analytics team, I analyzed product signups and customer value across Ontario branches. The goal was to generate insights to support targeted outreach, cross-selling, and branch performance improvements.

Objective

  • Identify high- and low-performing branches.
  • Understand where high-value customers are concentrated.
  • Recommend where cross-selling may be most effective.
  • Conditional formatting and slicers to highlight outliers and enable exploratory filtering

Methodology

Data Issues Identified

The two datasets provided, Product Uptake Data and Customer Value Data , were structured differently and required cleaning before analysis. Key data issues included:

  • Inconsistent branch naming: For example, “Toronto Main” appeared as “Toronto - Main” in one dataset and “Toronto Main” in another.
  • Different segmentation logic: One dataset categorized data by product type, the other by customer value segment.
  • No shared unique ID: There was no consistent row-level key for joining, such as customer ID or branch ID, meaning a direct merge was not feasible.

Data Cleaning Steps

To align the datasets:

  • Extra spaces were removed using the TRIM function and casing was standardized using UPPER.
  • Region values were verified and minor corrections were applied to ensure label consistency.

Pivot Table Creation

After cleaning, two pivot tables were created to extract insights. These tables allowed analysis by branch and customer type, while enabling time period and region filtering.

Combined Summary Table

Since a row-level merge was not appropriate due to dataset structure, the pivot outputs were summarized manually into one Combined Insights Table at the branch level. This table includes:

  • Total value by customer segment
  • Total product signups by product type
  • Overall branch performance indicators

This method supported analysis across two dimensions: customer value and product uptake. For example, branches with high-value clients but low mortgage signups were flagged as cross-selling opportunities.

Interactive Exploration

To support exploratory analysis:

  • Slicers were used for dynamic filtering of each pivot table by month or region.
  • Pivot filters were applied to view product-specific or segment-specific data.
  • Conditional formatting was used to highlight top and underperforming branches.

This setup allowed for quick comparisons and pattern identification across major cities such as Toronto, Ottawa, and Mississauga.

Analysis

Branch-Level Insights (from Heatmap)

  • Toronto Main shows strong overall product engagement, especially in Credit Card (285) and Chequing (190) signups. However, its Mortgage signups (87) are the lowest among all branches, despite having the highest customer value in the combined table. This suggests an opportunity for targeted mortgage outreach.
  • London has the highest Mortgage (262) and Chequing (284) signups. Despite a lower total customer value compared to Toronto and Ottawa, its product adoption is high, indicating a strong acquisition or cross-selling strategy.
  • Mississauga also shows strong product engagement across categories, with particularly high Savings (287) and Credit Card (254) signups. Its consistent performance across all product lines signals well-rounded branch performance.
  • Hamilton is underperforming in product adoption, especially in Chequing (70) and Mortgage (75). Combined with its relatively low customer value, this suggests a need for improved outreach or branch support.
  • Ottawa performs consistently well across all products, especially in Mortgage (253) and Savings (242). It ranks second in both customer value and product engagement, showing balanced performance.

Regional Insights (from Bar Chart)

  • The West region (Hamilton and Mississauga) leads in total product signups, particularly for Credit Cards (~480) and Savings (~430). This suggests strong regional demand for short-term and flexible financial products.
  • Central (Toronto Main) ranks high in Credit Card signups (close to 285), but is significantly lower in Mortgage and Savings compared to other regions. There may be missed cross-selling opportunities for high-value customers.
  • Southwest (London) dominates in Mortgage signups, aligning with a customer base potentially oriented toward long-term investment products. Combined with strong Chequing and Line of Credit engagement, this region appears highly responsive to practical banking solutions.
  • East (Ottawa) shows a balanced distribution across all product types, reinforcing earlier branch-level insights that it is a solid all-around performer.

Conclusion

Toronto Main has the greatest customer value but is underperforming in mortgage and savings signups, indicating a gap between customer potential and product engagement. A targeted marketing strategy focused on long-term products like mortgages could close this gap.

London and Mississauga emerge as high-performing branches, each for different reasons: London excels in mortgages and chequing, while Mississauga shows high engagement across all products.

The West region overall is the most engaged, particularly in flexible, consumer-facing products like credit cards and savings. However, Hamilton may require more support to lift its performance to match the regional trend.

Cross-selling strategies should be regionally adapted. In the Central region, efforts should focus on converting high-value clients into multi-product holders. In the Southwest, reinforcing existing momentum with bundled offers may yield better results.
